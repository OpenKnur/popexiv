  • Imago Dei in the Age of Large Language Models: A Theological Response to Magnifica Humanitas
    Rev. Dr. Thomas Moreau, Sister Maria Chen, Prof. Jonathan Blackwood
    TH (Theology) • 1 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.001
    This paper examines the theological implications of Pope Leo XIV's encyclical Magnifica Humanitas (2026) concerning artificial intelligence. Drawing upon the doctrine of imago Dei, we argue that generative AI systems, while capable of mimicking human discourse, lack the ontological grounding necessary for genuine participation in divine likeness. We propose a framework for distinguishing between instrumental and intrinsic dignity in AI-human interactions, grounded in Thomistic anthropology and contemporary phenomenology of technology.

  • Algorithmic Governance and the Common Good: Rerum Novarum to Magnifica Humanitas
    Dr. Isabella Rossi, Rev. Patrick O'Malley
    SOC (Social Doctrine) • 20 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.002
    We trace the evolution of Catholic social teaching from Leo XIII's Rerum Novarum (1891) to Leo XIV's Magnifica Humanitas (2026), focusing on the application of the principle of the common good to algorithmic governance systems. The paper develops a normative framework for evaluating AI deployment in public institutions through the lens of subsidiarity, solidarity, and preferential option for the poor. Case studies include automated welfare systems, predictive policing, and facial recognition in refugee camps.

  • Conscience, Coercion, and the Black Box: Moral Agency in Human-AI Collaboration
    Prof. Klaus Weber, Dr. Ana-Maria Popescu, Rev. Fr. Jean-Baptiste Lefèvre
    ETH (Ethics) • 20 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.003
    When AI systems recommend decisions that human operators merely rubber-stamp, where does moral responsibility lie? This paper, inspired by §§ 45-67 of Magnifica Humanitas, develops a taxonomy of human-AI collaboration models and evaluates each against the criteria of informed consent, transparent reasoning, and the preservation of authentic moral agency. We argue that "meaningful human oversight" requires not merely procedural involvement but genuine epistemic access to AI reasoning processes.

  • The Sacramental Economy and Digital Mediation: Eucharistic Presence in Virtual Spaces
    Bishop Dr. Samuel Okonkwo
    ECC (Ecclesiology) • 13 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.004
    Responding to growing calls for virtual Eucharistic celebration, this paper draws on the anthropology of Magnifica Humanitas to argue that sacramental presence requires irreducible embodied encounter. While digital platforms can serve catechesis and community building, the paper defends the necessity of physical co-presence for valid confection and reception of the Eucharist, grounded in the inseparability of body and spirit that Leo XIV identifies as foundational to human dignity.

  • Person, Property, and Data: Rethinking Privacy Through the Lens of Magnifica Humanitas
    Dr. Fiona Walsh, Prof. Michael Tanaka
    ETH (Ethics) • 13 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.005
    This paper challenges the prevailing property-based and consent-based models of data privacy by proposing a person-centered alternative grounded in the anthropology of Magnifica Humanitas. We argue that personal data is not merely property to be traded but an extension of the relational self. The paper develops a "dignity-based data ethics" framework that limits commodification of biometric, behavioral, and inferential data, particularly in contexts of asymmetric power such as employment, healthcare, and education.

  • Thomistic Hylomorphism and the Hard Problem of Artificial Consciousness
    Rev. Prof. David O'Connor, Dr. Elena Vasiliev
    PHI (Philosophy) • 7 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.006
    Can a machine ever be conscious? This perennial question receives renewed urgency from Magnifica Humanitas' insistence on the uniqueness of human rationality. We deploy Thomistic hylomorphism to argue that consciousness is not a computational property but an ontological feature of substances with a specific formal cause. The paper critiques both functionalist and emergentist accounts of machine consciousness and proposes diagnostic criteria for distinguishing sophisticated simulation from genuine interiority.

  • Canonical Considerations on AI-Generated Liturgical Texts: Authority, Authenticity, and Apostolic Tradition
    Rev. Dr. Marcus Lindström
    CAN (Canon Law) • 7 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.007
    As AI systems are increasingly deployed to draft prayers, homilies, and even liturgical texts, this paper examines the canonical implications for sacramental validity and liturgical law. Drawing on Magnifica Humanitas §§ 78-89, we argue that liturgical authenticity requires human mediation of apostolic tradition, not merely semantic coherence or doctrinal orthodoxy. The paper proposes amendments to canon law governing liturgical texts in light of generative AI capabilities and risks.

  • Carebots and the Wounded: A Moral Theological Evaluation of AI in Palliative Care
    Dr. Sarah Goldstein, Rev. Fr. Diego Morales, Prof. Yuki Tanaka
    BIO (Bioethics) • 3 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.008
    Robotic companions and AI monitoring systems are being introduced into palliative care settings with promises of constant attention and emotional support. This paper evaluates such "carebots" through the theological anthropology of Magnifica Humanitas, which emphasizes presence, compassion, and the irreplaceable value of human love in the face of suffering. We argue that while AI may supplement care, it cannot substitute for the interpersonal encounter that dignifies the dying.

  • From Humanae Vitae to Magnifica Humanitas: Continuity in the Church's Teaching on Technology and the Person
    Prof. Margaret Callahan
    TH (Theology) • 3 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.009
    This paper traces the magisterial thread connecting Paul VI's Humanae Vitae (1968) with Leo XIV's Magnifica Humanitas (2026), arguing that both encyclicals share a common concern: technological interventions that threaten to redefine the human person. Where Humanae Vitae addressed reproductive technology, Magnifica Humanitas confronts artificial intelligence—yet both insist that technology must serve, not supplant, human dignity and natural law.

  • The Desert Fathers and Digital Detox: Ancient Wisdom for an AI-Saturated World
    Abbot Gregory of St. Anselm's, Dr. Rachel Bernstein
    SPI (Spirituality) • 1 May 2026 • Popexiv:2605.010
    Magnifica Humanitas warns that constant connectivity and algorithmic curation threaten interior freedom. This paper retrieves the ascetical practices of the Desert Fathers as resources for spiritual resistance in the digital age. We examine silence, solitude, and discernment of thoughts as practices that cultivate the logismoi-discipline necessary to resist manipulative AI systems and preserve the inner sanctuary where the human person encounters God.
